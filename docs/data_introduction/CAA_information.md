# General Clean Air Act (CAA) Information

## 42 U.S.C. §7401 et seq. (1970)

- Comprehensive federal law that regulates air emissions from stationary and mobile sources. Authorizes EPA to establish National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS).
- First passed in 1963, but the operative version was created by the 1970 amendments (which also created the EPA) and substantially expanded in 1977 and 1990.
- Operates through **cooperative federalism**: the federal EPA sets standards and the states do most of the implementation and enforcement, subject to federal approval and backstop.
- The Act distinguishes between **mobile sources** (cars, trucks, planes) and **stationary sources** (power plants, factories, refineries, industrial boilers).

### NAAQS

- The conceptual foundation of the CAA. Set under **Section 109**, EPA designates a small set of "criteria pollutants" — currently ozone, particulate matter (PM2.5 and PM10), sulfur dioxide, nitrogen dioxide, carbon monoxide, and lead — and sets maximum allowable ambient concentrations for each.
- **"Primary" standards** protect public health; **"secondary" standards** protect welfare (crops, visibility, materials).
- NAAQS are about ambient air quality in a geographic area, not about any individual smokestack.
- Areas get classified as **attainment** (meeting the standard) or **nonattainment** (not meeting it), and that designation triggers very different regulatory regimes and carries real economic weight.

### State Implementation Plans (SIPs)

- **Section 110.** Each state writes a plan showing how it will achieve and maintain the NAAQS within its borders. This is the central mechanism and the place where federal standards become enforceable local requirements.
- If a state fails to produce an adequate plan, EPA can impose a **Federal Implementation Plan**.

### Sources of Pollution

- Section 112 of the Clean Air Act addresses emissions of **hazardous air pollutants (HAPs)**. A separate track for toxic air pollutants (the Act lists ~187), regulated through technology-based standards rather than ambient standards. 1990 CAA amendments revised section 112 to first require issuance of technology-based standards for major sources and certain area sources.
* Major sources: a stationary source or group of stationary sources that emit or have the potential to emit 10 tons per year or more of a hazardous air pollutant or 25 tons per year or more of a combination of hazardous air pollutants. 
    * For major sources: Section 112 requires EPA establish emission standards that require the maximum degree of reduction in emissions of hazardous pollutants. Standards commonly referred to as "maximum achievable control technology" or **MACT standards**. These are the **NESHAP** rules.
* Area source: stationary source that is not a major source.

### Stationary Sources

- Factories or chemical plants which must install pollution control equipment and meet emission limits under the CAA. 

- **New Source Review (NSR) and Prevention of Significant Deterioration (PSD).** A preconstruction permitting program for new or significantly modified large sources. Splits in two depending on the area:
    * **PSD** applies in **attainment areas** and requires "Best Available Control Technology."
    * **Nonattainment NSR** applies in dirty-air areas and is stricter, requiring the "Lowest Achievable Emission Rate" plus emission offsets.
    * The definition of what counts as a "modification" has been one of the most litigated questions in the whole Act.
    * Applies to facilities such as:
        * Coal-fired power plants
        * Plants that manufacture sulfuric and nitric acid
        * Glass manufacturing plants
        * Cement manufacturing plants
        * Petroleum refineries 

- **Air Toxics.** National Emission Standard for Hazardous Air Pollutants (NESHAP). Leaks, flares, and excess emissions from refineries, chemical plants, and other industries contain HAPs (cancer, birth defects, serious environmental impacts). 

- **New Source Performance Standards (NSPS).** Section 111. Nationally uniform, technology-based emission limits for categories of new and modified sources (separate from the area-based NAAQS logic). Section 111(d) extends this to existing sources for certain pollutants and has become the legal battleground for greenhouse gas regulation of power plants. Controls excess emissions of NOx, SO2, and particulate matter.

- **Title V Operating Permits.** Major sources must hold a single comprehensive operating permit consolidating all their CAA obligations. These permits, and the associated reporting, are a rich and underused data source.

## Research Connections

- **Attainment/nonattainment designations** are probably the single most useful construct for linking CAA regulation to community and economic outcomes. Designations change over time, are geographically explicit, and have documented effects on local industrial investment, employment, and housing. There is an established economics literature using them as a natural experiment.

- **Emissions data infrastructure** is substantial: EPA's National Emissions Inventory, the Air Markets Program Data (continuous emissions monitoring for power plants), and facility-level permit records. Note: the Toxics Release Inventory (TRI), often used for this kind of work, comes from a different statute (EPCRA) but pairs naturally with CAA data.

- **Environmental justice analysis** leans heavily on the spatial overlay of stationary sources (especially HAP sources) with demographic and economic data.

## Caveat on Legal Currency

The foundational architecture above is stable, but specific regulations and their legal status shift considerably — recent Supreme Court decisions on agency authority and ongoing administration-driven rule changes affect what is actually in force at any given moment. If the project needs the current regulatory state (active rules, latest NAAQS levels, pending litigation), verify against current sources rather than relying on this general framework.
